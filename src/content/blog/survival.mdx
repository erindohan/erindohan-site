On May 29, 2019, I was twenty-seven years old when I survived a ruptured brain aneurysm.

Eight hours of surgery. Three weeks in the hospital. And then, discharge — and the assumption, spoken and unspoken, that the hard part was over.

I didn't know how to tell anyone that it wasn't.

## What no one tells you about surviving

The medical team declared the outcome successful. The brain had been spared. The rupture was repaired. On paper, I was a success story. A miracle, even — people used that word.

What I was, actually, was profoundly disoriented. I had come back to a life that looked exactly like the one I'd left, and I couldn't figure out how to inhabit it. The person who had walked into the hospital on May 29 and the person who walked out three weeks later were not the same person. And yet everyone around me seemed to be waiting for the first one to return.

She didn't.

## The grief inside survival

What I didn't have language for, at the time, was grief.

I was alive. I was supposed to be grateful. And I was — genuinely, deeply grateful in a way that I carry with me still. But gratitude and grief are not opposites, and surviving something at twenty-seven does not mean you don't mourn the version of yourself that existed before it happened.

I mourned the certainty that had been ordinary to me. The unexamined confidence that my body was on my side. The ease of moving through the world without calculating risk, without the awareness of my own fragility that I now carried everywhere.

I grieved the self who didn't know what I now knew.

## Rebuilding is not the same as returning

Survival, I have come to understand, is not a return. It's a construction project.

You are not going back to who you were. You are figuring out who you are now, with this new information, in this altered body, with this knowledge that the worst-case scenario is not hypothetical.

That is a different project than recovery. Recovery implies an endpoint — a restoration of what was there before. What actually happens is something slower and stranger: learning how to trust your body again when it has betrayed you once. Learning what it means to be well when wellness is no longer something you take for granted.

That's been every day since.

## What I want to say to anyone still in it

If you are somewhere in the long middle — past the emergency, not yet whole — I want you to know that the middle is real. The disorientation is real. The strange grief of surviving something is real.

You are not doing it wrong because you're not better yet. You are not doing it wrong because gratitude and fear coexist in you. You are not doing it wrong because you mourn a version of yourself that no longer exists.

Survival isn't returning to who you were.

It's becoming someone new — slowly, imperfectly, one day at a time.

And that someone is allowed to take as long as they need.
