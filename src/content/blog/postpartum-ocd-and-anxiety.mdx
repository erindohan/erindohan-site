Nobody told me that what I'd experienced before pregnancy — the brain aneurysm, the miscarriage, the losses — would follow me into the postpartum period.

Nobody told me that trauma rewires the brain's alarm system. That pregnancy loss increases postpartum anxiety and intrusive thoughts. That the nervous system doesn't forget, even when you're holding a healthy baby and trying desperately to feel only gratitude.

I didn't know there was language for what I was experiencing. I didn't know I wasn't alone.

## What it looked like from the outside

From the outside, I looked fine. More than fine. I was managing. I was functioning. I was showing up for every appointment, every feeding, every milestone. I was doing all the things.

Postpartum OCD and anxiety are very good at hiding behind competence. Behind responsibility. Behind the kind of hypervigilance that looks, from a distance, like being a very attentive mother.

What it felt like from the inside was something else entirely.

## Intrusive thoughts are not who you are

Here is something I needed someone to tell me: intrusive thoughts are not evidence of who you are.

They are not wishes. They are not predictions. They are not moral failures.

Intrusive thoughts — the sudden, unwanted images and fears that flood the mind — are the brain's misguided way of trying to protect what you love most. The alarm system, overactivated by trauma, starts firing in situations that don't require it. It generates worst-case scenarios not because they're likely, but because it has learned that worst-case scenarios *can* happen.

For those of us who have lived through genuine medical emergencies, the alarm system has evidence. It has reason to stay on high alert. What it doesn't know how to do, without help, is calibrate.

## The thing that shifted something for me

I watched a documentary — *Witches* — and heard women describe their experiences with postpartum mental health in words I recognized immediately. The specificity of it. The way something that felt singular and shameful turned out to be shared and documented and real.

I remember the exact moment I thought: *this isn't who I am. This is what's happening to me.*

That distinction matters more than I can say. The illness as separate from the self. The symptoms as information, not identity.

## Healing is not linear, and you don't have to do it alone

If you are in the postpartum period and you are struggling — with intrusive thoughts, with anxiety that won't quiet, with fear that seems disproportionate to what anyone else can see — I want you to know:

This is real. It is recognized. It is treatable.

There are providers who specialize in perinatal mental health. There are medications that are safe during breastfeeding. There are therapists who won't tell you that you just need to sleep more or worry less.

You are not a bad mother. You are not dangerous. You are not broken.

You are a person whose alarm system got overwhelmed, and you deserve support in recalibrating it.
